Case Study · Interaction Design Capstone, Spring 2026

Redesigning the UC Berkeley EECS Website

A semester long group project where we picked apart UC Berkeley's EECS website, and figured out why basic things felt so hard to do, and rebuilt it. Here's what was broken, what we tried, and what user testing taught us.

Course
ITIS 4390 Capstone
Role
UX research, frontend dev, peer testing
Team
Group 15 — 4 members
Duration
~14 weeks
Tools
Figma, HTML/CSS/JS

The Problem

01 · Define

UC Berkeley's EECS site is the first place a prospective student, a current undergrad, or a visiting faculty member lands when they need something. And it makes a simple thing hard.

We started by going through it with Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and The site failed five of them. And these weren't cosmetic gripes. They were the kind of failures that actively get in the way: you can't tell what's important on a page, events are buried, and moving between sections feels like guesswork.

Below are the five we flagged, and why each one matters for the people using this site.

Nielsen #1

Visibility of System Status

Click any nav link and the page goes white for a second before the new one shows up. No hover, no loading spinner, nothing. You can't tell if your click actually did anything.

Nielsen #2

Match with the Real World

The nav is full of acronyms, BEARS, ASE, EECS, internal stuff. If you don't already know what they mean, the menu is basically gibberish.

Nielsen #3

User Control & Freedom

Once a dropdown opens, there's no obvious way to close it. Hovering away doesn't work. You have to click on dead space somewhere outside the menu, and most people don't think to try that.

Nielsen #4

Consistency & Standards

Some menu items open new pages, others show dropdowns. Some dropdowns close themselves, others don't. Every menu interaction makes you re learn the rules.

Nielsen #8

Aesthetic & Minimalist Design

The footer has redundant links, "For Students" and "For Undergrads," for example. The dropdowns and the "What's Happening" section are crammed with no breathing room.

Process

02 · Design

We worked from rough sketches to wireframes to a fully interactive Figma prototype, and tested at every stage so we weren't just designing in our heads.

01

Define a design concept

Before we touched a sketchpad, we wrote a quick concept statement so the whole team was on the same page. the Goal, fix the heuristic problems, lean hard on User Control & Freedom and Aesthetic & Minimalist Design, and make it feel calm and easy to scan on phone, tablet, and desktop.

That gave us five things to chase: more breathing room in dense sections, fewer acronyms, dropdowns that close cleanly, a visible loading state when you click a link, and a footer with fewer redundant links.

02

Low fidelity sketches

Pencil on paper for every key screen, desktop home, mobile home, dropdown menu, footer, and the loading screen we wanted to add. Sketching first kept us from liking the final decisions before we even knew if the structure made sense.

03

Mid fidelity wireframes (Figma)

From sketches we moved into Figma wireframes for desktop and mobile. This is where we locked in on the hero size, nav placement, event card grid, news layout, without getting stuck on colors or final imagery yet.

This is also where dark mode showed up. We wanted to design it explicitly instead of adding it on at the end, since the toggle ended up being one of the most tested features.

04

Add the loading-state pattern

The single biggest issue from the heuristic evaluation was that you'd click a nav link and just get a blank screen for a beat. So we designed an actual loading state, three animated dots and a "Taking you to [page name]…" message — so you always know your click registered and where you're headed.

05

Mobile from the start, not as an afterthought

Mobile got its own wireframes from day one. Single column layout, hamburger nav, and big touch targets for events and news cards. A bunch of testing decisions later, especially where the dark mode toggle should live, only made sense because we'd treated mobile as its own thing instead of squishing the desktop version down.

Testing & Iteration

03 · Validate

We ran two rounds of usability testing. Each one caught things we honestly hadn't seen coming, and forced changes we wouldn't have made on our own.

Round 1: Wireframe testing (4 peers, think-aloud)

Three tasks: find a news article, RSVP for an event, switch to dark mode. Testers worked through the wireframes on their own with no help from us.

Finding 1 · Mobile

Dark mode toggle was in the wrong place on mobile

What happened
A few testers just couldn't find the toggle on mobile. One literally said: "I'd want it placed as an option in the hamburger menu as a setting."
Why it happened
On phones, people look for site settings inside the hamburger menu, not floating in the header bar. Our desktop pattern didn't carry over.
What we changed
Moved the toggle inside the hamburger menu on mobile so it lives where people actually look for stuff like that.
Finding 2 · Mobile

People kept missing the RSVP button on mobile entirely

What happened
Testers tapped the event thumbnail or the event card itself, never the actual RSVP button. The button was visible, just not where people instinctively went.
Why it happened
On a phone, the whole card looks tappable. Putting a separate button inside a tappable looking card just creates confusion.
What we changed
Removed the standalone RSVP button on mobile and made the whole event card tappable. The button stays on desktop where mouse hover makes it work.
Finding 3 · Desktop

"RSVP / Details" tripped people up

What happened
One tester spent a while looking for a separate booking button, they read "Details" as a different action than RSVP.
Why it happened
The slash made it look like two things. People scan, and "RSVP / Details" got read as "RSVP or Details, pick one."
What we changed
Just made it "RSVP" on desktop. Less ambiguous, cleaner button.

Round 2: High-fidelity prototype testing (3 participants)

After Round 1's revisions, we tested the interactive Figma prototype with new people on the same task set.

I'd naturally go to the Academics tab for an event RSVP, that's where I assume school stuff lives. P1, on Task 2
Finding 4 · Information architecture

One path to RSVP wasn't enough

What happened
P1 made 6 misclicks hunting for RSVP under Academics before finding it on the home page. P2 and P3 had no problem but P1's behavior is exactly the kind of thing the site should support, not punish.
What we changed
Added an Events / RSVP entry under Academics, on top of the home page one. More than one path to the same task = less friction for people who think differently.
Finding 5 · Form

The RSVP form was missing a submit button

What happened
P3 filled out the entire form, then had nowhere to go. Genuine prototype gap on our end, not a design choice.
What we changed
Added a labeled Submit RSVP button. Also split full name into first and last fields based on the same tester's suggestion.
Finding 6 · Visual hierarchy

The search page felt empty

What happened
P1 said the page felt visually sparse and that nothing pulled their eye toward the search bar.
What we changed
Wrapped the search bar in a styled hero section so it's clearly the main thing on the page. Lower-priority content drops below the fold.

Final Product

04 · Build

After all that, we built the redesign as a working static site in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The screenshots below are from the real thing.

Home page

Light and dark share the same components. Toggle is in the top right utility bar.

Search — typeahead with no-results state

Search is client side typeahead, so results show up as you type. Matched terms get highlighted, results are grouped by source page, and we explicitly handle the empty case.

RSVP form validation and submission

This is the form Round 2 testing flagged. Submit button is in (Finding 5), and inline validation catches problems before submit so people aren't surprised by what's required.

Across the site — does the design hold up?

A redesign that only looks good on the home page is half a redesign. These three interior pages show whether the components, type, and color choices hold up across longer, denser, and form templates.

What I'd Do Differently

05 · Reflect

Looking back, here's what I'd do differently next time:

Test mobile sooner

Almost all the big revisions in Round 1 were mobile specific. If we'd built mobile prototypes first instead of after the desktop ones were already solid, we'd have caught the dark mode and RSVP issues a week earlier.

More than 3 testers in Round 2

Round 2 had 3 testers and one of them flagged a real issue, the RSVP-in Academics thing that the other two didn't. With only 3 people, that's the difference between a real problem and an outlier, we just didn't have enough data to know.

Bigger paper sketches, sooner

We jumped to Figma kind of fast. More time on paper sketches with the team in one room would've surfaced layout disagreements before we'd already invested time in mid fi components.

Always have multiple paths to the same thing

The single path RSVP flow was a real lesson. Different people hold different mental models about where things should live. Building redundant entry points isn't clutter, it's just respecting that not everyone thinks the same way.
